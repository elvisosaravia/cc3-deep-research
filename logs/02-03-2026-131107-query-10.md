# Research Report: State of Antibiotic Resistance and New Drug Development Pipelines

**Date:** March 2, 2026 | **Sources consulted:** 7

## Executive Summary

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) represents one of the most urgent global health crises, with projections showing 39 million direct deaths between 2025 and 2050—approximately three deaths every minute. The WHO's 2025 pipeline analysis reveals a "dual crisis of scarcity and lack of innovation": the number of antibacterials in clinical development fell from 97 in 2023 to 90 as of February 2025, with only 15 deemed innovative and only 5 effective against WHO's "critical" priority pathogens. The preclinical pipeline is more active (232 programs across 148 groups worldwide), but 90% are conducted by small companies with fewer than 50 employees, raising viability concerns. Two notable first-in-class antibiotics were approved recently—zoliflodacin for gonorrhea and gepotidacin for urinary tract infections—but these represent incremental progress against a problem projected to cost $1–3.4 trillion in GDP losses annually by 2030.

## Key Findings

### A Staggering Global Burden
AMR has claimed over 36 million lives since 1990, with 1.14 million deaths directly attributable to bacterial AMR in 2021 alone. A landmark Lancet study projects 39 million direct AMR deaths between 2025 and 2050, with annual deaths rising 67.5% from 1.14 million (2021) to 1.91 million (2050). The burden falls disproportionately on low- and middle-income countries, with Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia having the highest rates. Deaths among those 70+ will account for 65.9% of all AMR deaths by 2050, while deaths among children under 5 have declined 50% since 1990—reflecting improved pediatric care but growing vulnerability in aging populations. AMR is projected to reduce global GDP by $1–3.4 trillion per year by 2030.

### A Shrinking and Insufficiently Innovative Pipeline
The WHO's October 2025 analysis paints a bleak picture: the clinical pipeline fell from 97 agents in 2023 to 90 as of February 2025. Of these, 50 are traditional antibacterial agents and 40 are non-traditional approaches (bacteriophages, antibodies, microbiome-modulating). Only 15 are deemed innovative, and just 5 target WHO "critical" priority pathogens. The preclinical pipeline is more active with 232 programs, but 90% are at small companies with under 50 employees—vulnerable to funding gaps. Recent approvals include zoliflodacin (first-in-class for gonorrhea) and gepotidacin (first-in-class for UTIs), but without sustained investment, the pipeline is expected to decline further from 2026 onward as existing Phase II/III funding expires.

### A Broken Economic Model
The fundamental challenge is economic: the "Antibiotic Paradox" means new drugs must be used sparingly to prevent resistance, making it nearly impossible to recoup the estimated $1 billion R&D investment. Most development is conducted by small companies (90% of preclinical programs) that face bankruptcy risk. G7 and EU countries are discussing "pull incentives" including Market Entry Rewards and subscription models (the UK's "Netflix model," the proposed U.S. PASTEUR Act), but implementation remains slow. Interventions could make a major difference: the Lancet study estimates that in a "better care" scenario, 92 million cumulative AMR deaths could be averted through improved infection treatment and antibiotic access.

## Areas of Uncertainty

- Whether pull incentive mechanisms will be implemented at sufficient scale to reverse the pipeline decline.
- The viability of non-traditional approaches (phage therapy, mAbs) at regulatory and commercial scale.
- How the projected shift in AMR burden toward elderly populations will affect healthcare systems already strained by aging demographics.
- Whether the 92 million preventable deaths estimate from the "better care" scenario is achievable given current health system capacity in LMICs.
