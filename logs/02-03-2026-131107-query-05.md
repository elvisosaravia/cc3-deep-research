## App Output (Gemini 3 Flash + Exa)

In the decade since the 2015 Paris Agreement, international climate policy has shifted from a "top-down" target-setting model to a "bottom-up" framework of continuous assessment and ratcheting ambition. This evolution has successfully lowered the trajectory of global warming from a projected **4°C** in 2015 to approximately **2.6°C–3.1°C** today.

Despite significant progress in clean energy deployment, the 2023 Global Stocktake and recent 2024 reports confirm a persistent "implementation gap." Current national plans (NDCs) remain insufficient to meet the 1.5°C limit, and annual greenhouse gas emissions continue to reach record levels.

## Key Findings

### 1. The "Ratchet Mechanism" and NDC Evolution
*   **Third-Generation NDCs (NDC 3.0):** As of late 2024, countries are submitting "3.0" versions of their plans. Recent submissions cover approximately 45% of global emissions.
*   **Net-Zero Mainstreaming:** Over 150 countries have pledged to reach net-zero by mid-century.

### 2. The First Global Stocktake (COP28)
*   **The UAE Consensus:** For the first time in nearly 30 years, the final agreement explicitly called for **"transitioning away from fossil fuels"**.
*   **Renewable Energy Targets:** COP28 agreed to **tripling renewable energy capacity** and doubling energy efficiency improvements by 2030.

### 3. Breakthroughs in Climate Finance (COP29)
*   **NCQG Agreement:** Countries tripled the annual finance goal for developing nations from $100 billion to **$300 billion by 2035**.
*   **Loss and Damage:** A dedicated fund was operationalized at COP28 and began receiving initial capital pledges.

### 4. Technological vs. Political Friction
*   **Renewable Boom:** Solar deployment is 15x higher than predicted in 2015; clean power accounts for **41% of global electricity generation**.
*   **Geopolitical Challenges:** Progress threatened by diplomatic shifts, most notably U.S. participation uncertainty.

# Research Report: Progress on International Climate Agreements Since the Paris Accord

**Date:** March 2, 2026 | **Sources consulted:** 7

## Executive Summary

A decade after the Paris Agreement, international climate diplomacy has achieved notable structural milestones—the first Global Stocktake, a tripled finance commitment, and an unprecedented call to transition away from fossil fuels—while falling critically short on emissions reductions. Current NDC pledges, if fully implemented, would limit warming to 2.3–2.5°C by 2100, far exceeding the 1.5°C target. Only 108 countries representing 71% of global emissions had submitted new NDCs by November 2025, achieving less than 14% of the additional reductions needed. The U.S. withdrawal from the Paris Agreement, effective January 2026, further undermines collective ambition, while global emissions rose 1.3% in 2024 to record levels.

## Key Findings

### Structural Progress: From Dubai to Baku
COP28 in Dubai (2023) delivered the first Global Stocktake and the landmark "UAE Consensus" explicitly calling for transitioning away from fossil fuels—a first in 30 years of UN climate negotiations. Parties agreed to triple renewable energy capacity and double energy efficiency by 2030. A Loss and Damage Fund was operationalized with initial pledges of approximately $430 million. COP29 in Baku (2024) established the New Collective Quantified Goal (NCQG), tripling annual climate finance commitments from $100 billion to $300 billion by 2035, and finalized Article 6 carbon market standards. However, estimated annual climate finance needs exceed $600 billion by 2030—double the pledged amount.

### The NDC Ambition Gap
As of November 2025, 108 countries representing 71% of global emissions had submitted new NDCs, but the ambition falls far short. Current unconditional NDCs would reduce emissions by only 3.2 GtCO2e by 2035, leaving a 28 GtCO2e gap to the 1.5°C pathway. Only 15 of 108 countries (14%) increased their 2030 pledges, and notably no wealthy, high-emitting nations did so. The combined NDC 3.0 submissions by September 2025 would reduce 2035 global emissions by only 15%, whereas a reduction of 55% would be needed for 1.5°C. Country-specific 2035 targets include: UK (81% from 1990), EU (66.25–72.5%), China (7–10% from peak), Japan (60% from 2013), and the U.S. pledge of 61–66% from 2005 levels (now moot after withdrawal).

### The U.S. Withdrawal and Geopolitical Fractures
On January 27, 2026, the U.S. withdrawal from the Paris Agreement became effective. The Trump administration further announced withdrawal from the UNFCCC itself, the foundational treaty underlying all international climate cooperation. This removes the world's second-largest emitter from the framework entirely, canceling approximately 0.1°C of warming improvement from new NDCs. 2025 ranked among the three hottest years ever recorded, and exceedance of 1.5°C is now considered very likely within the next decade.

## Areas of Uncertainty

- Whether COP30 (November 2025 in Belem) can generate sufficient new pledges to close the ambition gap.
- The $300 billion NCQG falls far short of the $600 billion+ annual needs identified by developing nations ($2.8 trillion reported by 41 countries).
- The long-term impact of U.S. UNFCCC withdrawal on the multilateral climate framework remains unclear.
- Renewable energy deployment (solar 15x above 2015 predictions) outpaces policy ambition, creating tension between technological optimism and political reality.
